# The weekly dashboard every group practice owner needs

*By Bailey Bryant, Psy.D.  ·  Hello Studio Journal  ·  ~9 min read*

---

## I built a spreadsheet that almost broke my practice

It started innocently. One tab for inquiries. One tab for follow-ups. One tab for therapist assignments. A column for *Date of First Response*, because I wanted to know how fast we were getting back to people. Then a column for *Acceptable Methods of Contact*, because what if someone preferred email and we kept calling? Then a column for *If referred, can we thank the referral source?*, because, well, gratitude is a discipline.

By the time I'd finished, my intake-management spreadsheet had eighteen columns and three tabs. It tracked everything — client name, date of contact, time of day, where the inquiry came from, what the presenting concern was, who responded, when they responded, what the outcome was, which therapist got assigned, whether the first appointment was attended, and on, and on, and on.

It was, on paper, beautiful.

In practice, it was a swamp.

Nobody filled it in consistently. The columns I cared most about (response time, conversion rate) were buried in the columns I'd added in a moment of optimism (*Time of Day*, *If Other, describe*). When I sat down on a Friday to look at how the week had gone, I'd open the sheet, scroll horizontally for ten seconds, scroll back, and close it. Whatever I'd hoped to learn from that spreadsheet was drowning in everything else it was trying to tell me.

If you run a group practice, I suspect you have one of these spreadsheets too. Or you have something worse — a Google Doc, a notebook, a memory. Or you have nothing, which means you're navigating by feel, which is a perfectly valid strategy until it isn't.

This essay is about what I learned when I finally threw mine out.

## The problem isn't tracking too little. It's tracking too much.

Here is the trap most practice owners fall into: when something feels off in the practice, we add another column to the spreadsheet.

Caseloads feel uneven? Add a *Current Caseload* column. Notes are running late? Add a *Notes Due This Week* column. Revenue feels lumpy? Add a *Billed This Week / Collected This Week* split.

Each addition makes sense in the moment. None of them, individually, is the mistake. The mistake is that with each new column, the cost of *looking at the spreadsheet at all* gets a tiny bit higher — until one Friday you don't open it.

What I needed wasn't more data. I needed less data, organized so I'd actually look.

So I asked myself a different question: if I could only see six numbers about the practice each week — and I had to make decisions from those six alone — which would I choose?

After a year of trial and error, here are the six I landed on.

## The six numbers that actually tell you whether the practice is healthy

**1. New client inquiries.** Every first-touch contact this week — phone, form, email, referral, walk-in, whatever. This is your top-of-funnel reality check. A dip here doesn't show up as a problem until weeks later, when your caseload thins out and you're scrambling. Watch this number, and you'll see the slowdown coming.

**2. Inquiry → intake conversion percentage.** Intakes scheduled this week ÷ inquiries this week. Yes, the math is fuzzy — some of this week's intakes came from last week's inquiries. That's fine. You're tracking the *ratio* over time, not matching individual people. If you're consistently below 50%, something is leaking. Maybe your response time is too slow. Maybe your fees aren't landing in the consult call. Maybe your intake email is too long and people drop. The number won't tell you which one. But it will tell you that the leak is there.

**3. Booked hours vs. productivity goal.** Total clinical hours booked across your team divided by your weekly goal (six clinicians × twenty-five hours = a hundred and fifty, for example). This is the number that connects caseload to revenue. When booked hours sag, it usually isn't because clients aren't there. It's because cancellations aren't being filled, or a clinician is ramping slower than expected, or the schedule has structural gaps you stopped noticing.

**4. Weekly revenue collected.** Not billed. *Collected.* The cash that actually hit your account this week. Billed revenue is optimism. Collected revenue is reality. Watching this weekly is what catches insurance lag, billing errors, and cash-flow surprises before they become a phone call to the bookkeeper at 9pm on a Sunday.

**5. Clinician caseload health.** What percentage of your clinicians are at or near full caseload, versus under? You define what "full" means — usually somewhere between twenty-two and twenty-eight client hours a week, depending on your model. The reason I track this and not "average caseload" is that averages lie. If I have one clinician at thirty hours and one at ten, the average is twenty — which sounds fine and is, in fact, a five-alarm fire. One person is heading toward burnout. The other is underutilized and probably worried about it. Caseload health makes both visible.

**6. Notes completion percentage.** Notes finished on time this week ÷ notes due this week. "On time" is whatever your policy says — for us it's within forty-eight hours. Late notes are the canary in the coal mine for clinician overwhelm, documentation drag, and billing delays. They're also a compliance issue waiting to happen. If this number drifts under 75%, you need a conversation with your team this week, not next month.

That's it. Six numbers. Fifteen minutes once a week to log them.

## When the dashboard stops being enough

There will come a moment — and you will know when it arrives — when this spreadsheet is no longer cutting it. Maybe your team is getting bigger and the per-clinician detail starts mattering more. Maybe your intake volume is high enough that you need automated follow-up sequences and not just a column tracking who got a callback. Maybe you're making a hire, and the data you need to defend that hire to yourself isn't in here.

That's when you graduate.

For us, that meant moving clinical metrics into [PracticeVital](https://practicevital.com), where I can see caseload, productivity, and notes completion broken down per clinician, in real time, without anyone having to type a number into a sheet. And it meant moving intake tracking into [TherapyFlow](https://therapyflowcrm.com), where every inquiry has a status, every conversion is automatic, and I'm not the bottleneck for follow-ups anymore.

I am not telling you to use those specific tools. I'm telling you that the day will come when a spreadsheet — *any* spreadsheet, including this one — won't be enough, and that day is good news. It means you've outgrown the system. It means you have enough complexity to justify investing in real software. It means the practice is winning.

Until then? Six numbers. Fifteen minutes. Once a week. Every week.

## What I want for you

I want you to stop opening a spreadsheet on Friday and closing it again because it's too overwhelming to face. I want you to know, before the end of any given month, whether your practice is trending healthy or trending sideways. I want you to make hiring decisions based on something other than vibes. I want you to spot the soft spots in your business while they're still soft — not after they've calcified into a real problem.

The owners I've watched thrive aren't the ones with the fanciest dashboards. They're the ones who actually *look* at the numbers, week after week, before the numbers become a phone call from the IRS or a clinician resignation letter or a cash-flow scare.

Six numbers. That's the price of admission.
